For the more original, "tantalizing" piece, I suggest you build a compact experiment around CompuFlair's video "This physics idea might be the next generation of machine learning" (which gestures toward active inference and the free‑energy principle), supported by a relatively lightweight, tutorial‑style paper.

Chosen references

  • Conceptual reference: "A step‑by‑step tutorial on active inference and its application to…", which walks through the basics of active inference and the free‑energy principle in a relatively accessible way.

4.1 Project concept: 1D active‑inference‑style agent

Instead of implementing a full, modern active‑inference stack, your goal is to code a small agent that:

  • Lives on a 1D line with a simple state (e.g., position).
  • Has a preferred state distribution (e.g., "likes" to stay near the center).
  • Perceives noisy observations and chooses actions that minimize a simple free‑energy‑like objective (surprise relative to its preferences).

You then relate this to CompuFlair's "physics idea" by explicitly mapping your cost function back to a free‑energy or entropy interpretation, even if in simplified form.

4.2 Implementation scope in Rust/Mojo

A reasonable scope that remains portfolio‑ready but not overwhelming:

  • Implement the agent's generative model and recognition model in Rust: a simple Gaussian belief over state with a prior centered at the preferred region; observations are noisy positions.
  • Define a free‑energy‑like objective (e.g., negative log likelihood + KL term to the preferred distribution), and implement gradient‑based updates to beliefs and/or actions per time‑step.
  • Provide a tiny visualization (text‑mode plot in the terminal or a basic HTML/SVG output) showing how the agent moves over time toward its preferred region under noise.
  • Optionally, provide a Mojo version to emphasize your comfort with experimental high‑performance languages.
