#### Sample 1 — signal_integrity (278 words)

**Instruction:**

> A TDR sweep on a 75-ohm controlled-impedance trace (80 mm length, coaxial cable plant) shows impedance rising from 75 ohm at the launch to 82 ohm at 30 mm, then gradually increasing to 91 ohm by 80 mm. The trace is on a 6-layer PCB routed on layer 3 with a ground reference on layer 4. What does this drift pattern indicate?

**Response:**

This is a clear fault detection finding: the monotonic impedance rise from 75 ohm to 91 ohm across 80 mm indicates a systematic dielectric thickness variation along the trace route. The trace is not meeting its 75-ohm target — it exceeds the typical +/-10% tolerance (82.5 ohm max) beyond the 30 mm mark.

The physics is direct. For a stripline on layer 3 referenced to ground on layer 4, Z0 is proportional to sqrt(L/C), and both L and C depend on the dielectric height h between the trace and reference plane. Specifically, for a stripline, Z0 increases approximately linearly with h. The gradual rise from 75 to 91 ohm (+21%) implies the dielectric thickness between layers 3 and 4 is increasing by roughly 20% across the board from the trace launch point to the far end.

This pattern has two common causes: (1) Prepreg thickness variation during PCB lamination — if the press platens are not perfectly parallel, the dielectric compresses more on one side than the other, creating a wedge-shaped cross-section. A 20% thickness variation over 80 mm is severe and indicates a lamination process problem. (2) Copper density variation in adjacent layers affecting resin flow during lamination — areas with less copper fill allow more resin to flow, increasing local dielectric thickness.

Severity is high. At 91 ohm, the reflection coefficient relative to a 75-ohm source is rho = (91-75)/(91+75) = 0.096, and this distributed mismatch creates cumulative return loss degradation across the entire trace. Request the PCB fabricator's cross-section report at three locations along this trace to confirm the dielectric thickness variation, and review the copper balance on layers 2 and 4 near this route.

#### Sample 2 — boundary_scan (229 words)

**Instruction:**

> A boundary scan test system reads the following IDCODE values from device 5 in an 8-device chain over 6 consecutive reads: 0x04A00093, 0x04A00093, 0x44A00093, 0x04A00093, 0x04A00093, 0x44A00093. All other devices return stable values. The TCK frequency is 15 MHz. What does this pattern suggest?

**Response:**

Bit 30 of device 5's IDCODE is intermittently flipping — appearing as set (0x44A00093) in reads 3 and 6. This is a fault detection finding: the JTAG data path has marginal signal integrity at device 5's position in the chain.

The IDCODE register is mask-programmed in silicon and physically cannot change value. Any variation in read-back is a data capture artifact. At 15 MHz TCK, the clock period is only 66.7 ns. In an 8-device chain, TDO data from device 5 must propagate through devices 6-8 before reaching the test system's TDI receiver. Each device adds approximately 8-12 ns of propagation delay, and uncontrolled-impedance traces between devices contribute additional delay from signal integrity degradation — ringing, reflections at impedance discontinuities, and crosstalk from adjacent signals.

The intermittent nature (2 out of 6 reads affected) indicates the timing margin is very small — the TDO edge lands within a few hundred picoseconds of the TCK capture window. Temperature changes, supply voltage variation, or even adjacent signal activity can tip the result either way.

This is a high-priority finding for production test reliability. At 15 MHz, the chain is operating beyond its reliable bandwidth given the routing quality. Recommended actions: reduce TCK to 5 MHz as an immediate fix, then investigate adding series termination (22-33 ohm) at each device's TDO output and improving trace impedance control on the JTAG bus routing.

### FI

#### Sample 1 — signal_integrity (514 words)

**Instruction:**

> Voltage margining tests on a digital processing board sweep the 1.0V core supply from 0.85V to 1.15V in 10 mV steps while running a memory-intensive functional test pattern. The results show: PASS from 0.90V to 1.10V (symmetric 100 mV margin at nominal 1.0V). However, a second board from the same production lot shows: PASS from 0.94V to 1.10V only — the low-voltage margin is reduced by 40 mV. Both boards pass all tests at the nominal 1.0V. The processor is a 7nm CMOS ASIC clocked at 800 MHz, and the memory-intensive test involves DDR4 read/write operations at 2400 MT/s. Isolate why board 2 has reduced low-voltage margin.

**Response:**

The reduced low-voltage margin on board 2 indicates that the critical timing path in the processor-to-DDR4 interface has less slack on this board, and the slack deficit manifests only when the supply voltage is reduced below the threshold where timing closure fails.

The most likely root cause is signal integrity variation in the DDR4 data path — specifically, higher insertion loss or worse impedance matching on board 2 compared to board 1. This is a board-level manufacturing variation, not a component defect.

The physics connecting supply voltage to timing margin is rooted in CMOS gate delay: t_pd = C_load * V_DD / (k * (V_DD - V_th)^alpha), where V_th is the transistor threshold voltage, C_load is the output capacitance, and alpha is approximately 1.3 for short-channel devices. As V_DD decreases, gate delay increases non-linearly. At 1.0V, a 7nm ASIC might have typical gate delay of 15 ps; at 0.90V, this increases to approximately 19 ps (27% slower). The memory interface timing budget must accommodate this slowdown.

Board 2's failure at 0.94V instead of 0.90V means its timing path is approximately 4 mV * (dt_pd/dV) slower than board 1 at any given voltage. The 40 mV difference in margin threshold translates to roughly 2-3 ps of additional path delay on board 2. At 2400 MT/s DDR4 (416 ps unit interval), this 2-3 ps consumes the setup time margin that board 1 has but board 2 lacks.

Possible sources of the additional delay on board 2:
1. PCB trace impedance variation — if board 2 has 5-10% lower impedance due to dielectric thickness variation (within PCB manufacturer's tolerance), reflections at impedance discontinuities add settling time
2. Via stub length variation — board 2 may have slightly longer via stubs in the DDR4 routing, creating resonant notches in the channel transfer function
3. Solder joint quality — marginal joints on DDR4 data pins add contact resistance and parasitic inductance

Verification steps:

1. Run the voltage margin test with the DDR4 data rate reduced to 2133 MT/s. If board 2's low margin improves to match board 1, the root cause is confirmed as a DDR4 timing path issue. The wider unit interval at 2133 MT/s (469 ps) provides the additional margin.

2. Perform TDR measurements on the DDR4 data traces on both boards. Compare impedance profiles — look for impedance deviations greater than 5 ohm from the target 50 ohm. Any impedance discontinuity on board 2 not present on board 1 is a candidate root cause.

3. Measure the DDR4 data eye diagram on both boards at 1.0V nominal using a compliance probe. Compare eye height and eye width. Board 2 should show a measurably smaller eye (3-5% reduction would be consistent with the 40 mV margin loss).

4. Review the PCB fabrication data for board 2's panel. Check the cross-section report for dielectric thickness — a variation of 0.5 mil on the reference layer spacing changes trace impedance by approximately 5%, which is sufficient to account for the timing difference. If fabrication variation is the cause, tighten the PCB impedance specification from +/- 10% to +/- 7%.

#### Sample 2 — mixed_signal (474 words)

**Instruction:**

> A precision 14-bit ADC on a data acquisition board shows the following INL (integral nonlinearity) measurements: INL is within +/- 0.3 LSB across most of the code range, but there is a sharp spike to +3.8 LSB at code 8192 (mid-scale), with elevated INL of +1.5 to +2.5 LSB persisting from codes 8000 to 8400. The DNL (differential nonlinearity) at code 8192 is +2.1 LSB. The ADC uses a successive approximation register (SAR) architecture with a capacitive DAC. All other codes show DNL within +/- 0.3 LSB. What is the root cause of the mid-scale nonlinearity error?

**Response:**

The root cause is a mismatch in the most significant bit (MSB) capacitor of the SAR ADC's internal capacitive DAC. Code 8192 is exactly mid-scale for a 14-bit ADC (2^13), which is the transition point where the MSB capacitor switches — making this the most sensitive code to MSB capacitor error.

In a SAR ADC, the internal DAC reconstructs the analog input using a binary-weighted capacitor array. The MSB capacitor (C_MSB) should be exactly equal to the sum of all lower-bit capacitors: C_MSB = C_MSB-1 + C_MSB-2 + ... + C_LSB + C_termination. If C_MSB deviates from this ideal by delta_C, the DAC output at mid-scale has an error of V_error = V_ref * delta_C / (2 * C_total).

The +3.8 LSB INL spike at mid-scale translates to V_error = 3.8 * V_ref / 2^14 = 3.8 * V_LSB. For a 14-bit ADC with 4.096V reference, V_LSB = 250 uV, so the error is approximately 950 uV. This corresponds to a capacitor mismatch of delta_C/C_total = 3.8 / 2^13 = 0.046%, or about 460 ppm — within the range of typical silicon process variations for capacitor matching.

The elevated INL from codes 8000-8400 (approximately 400 codes around mid-scale) indicates that the mismatch also affects the transition region where the MSB and MSB-1 capacitors interact. As the SAR converges through the bits surrounding mid-scale, the MSB error propagates into the subsequent bit decisions, creating a region of degraded linearity rather than a single-code anomaly.

The +2.1 LSB DNL at code 8192 confirms the MSB error: DNL measures the difference between actual and ideal step sizes, and a large DNL at a single major transition code is the hallmark of binary-weighted DAC mismatch.

This is fundamentally a silicon-level issue, not a board-level defect. The ADC die's capacitor matching is determined during fabrication.

Verification steps:

1. Measure the INL/DNL on a second ADC from the same lot. If the mid-scale spike appears at similar magnitude, it indicates a systematic process issue affecting the lot. If the magnitude differs significantly, it is within-lot random variation.

2. Check the ADC datasheet for specified INL limits. A +3.8 LSB INL may exceed the device's guaranteed specification (typically +/- 1-2 LSB for a 14-bit SAR ADC). If it does, the component is out-of-specification and should be rejected or returned to the supplier.

3. If the ADC supports internal calibration or offset/gain trim registers, run the calibration routine. Some SAR ADCs have built-in capacitor mismatch calibration that can reduce mid-scale INL to below +/- 1 LSB.

4. If software correction is acceptable, build an INL correction lookup table by measuring the ADC against a precision voltage source. Apply the correction in firmware: corrected_code = raw_code - INL_table[raw_code]. This can reduce the effective INL to below +/- 0.5 LSB across the full code range, provided the INL is stable over temperature and time.

#### Sample 1 — thermal (418 words)

**Instruction:**

> Here is the Icc quiescent current data for U7 from our latest production lot. We collect this on every board at ICT. Wanted your routine analysis.

Icc (quiescent supply current) — U7, 3.3V LDO regulator:
  Historical baseline (first 2,000 boards): mean = 42.3 mA, sigma = 2.8 mA
  Current lot (last 350 boards): mean = 65.1 mA, sigma = 2.9 mA
  Shift: +22.8 mA (54% above historical mean)
  Sigma unchanged — distribution shifted, not broadened
  All readings within current test limits (10 mA to 150 mA)

**Response:**

This Icc distribution data contains a significant systematic shift that warrants investigation despite passing current test limits.

Detection: The quiescent supply current for U7 has shifted from a historical mean of 42.3 mA to 65.1 mA — an increase of 22.8 mA, representing a 54% rise. Critically, the standard deviation is unchanged (2.8 mA vs 2.9 mA), which tells us the distribution has translated upward without broadening. This is a rigid shift of the entire population, not a tail effect or increased scatter. While all readings technically pass the test limits of 10-150 mA, this shift is physically meaningful and should not be ignored simply because it falls within wide test limits.

Isolation: The stable sigma is the key diagnostic clue. If the increased current were caused by random defects — such as solder shorts, contamination, or damaged devices — we would expect sigma to increase because defects affect units randomly and by varying amounts. Instead, the constant sigma with shifted mean indicates a deterministic, systematic cause that affects every unit equally. The physics narrows this to two possibilities. First, a component lot change: if the 3.3V LDO regulator silicon was sourced from a different wafer lot or fabrication revision, the internal bias currents and quiescent operating point may have shifted. The 22.8 mA increase represents additional power dissipation of P = 22.8 mA * 3.3V = 75 mW, which is significant for thermal budget but may be within the device specification. Second, a design revision change: if the board revision changed passive component values in the LDO feedback network, the output voltage or operating point could shift, changing quiescent current.

The 54% increase is large enough to affect thermal performance. The additional 75 mW raises the junction temperature by delta_T = P * theta_JA, which for a typical SOT-223 package (theta_JA approximately 65 C/W) means approximately 5 C hotter. This reduces thermal margin and could trigger failures under worst-case ambient conditions.

Recommended action: Pull the component date codes from the current lot and compare against the baseline lot. If the LDO supplier or lot changed, request the supplier's characterization data for the new lot to verify the Icc shift is within their device specification. Tighten the test limits to flag this shift — the current 10-150 mA window is too wide to catch a 54% shift. A limit of mean +/- 4 sigma from the historical baseline (approximately 31-54 mA) would have flagged this immediately. Update the thermal analysis to account for the additional 75 mW dissipation.

#### Sample 1 — thermal (403 words)

**Instruction:**

> A surface mount assembly line is experiencing a 6% defect rate on 0201 resistor placements, detected by post-reflow AOI. The defects are classified as 'tombstoning' — one end of the resistor lifts off the pad during reflow, standing vertically. The tombstoning rate was < 0.5% for the past 6 months. A new reflow oven was installed 2 weeks ago. The component supplier, solder paste, stencil, and pick-and-place machine are unchanged. Is there a component lot with inconsistent metallization causing preferential wetting on one terminal?

**Response:**

This is not a component metallization defect. The timing correlation with the new reflow oven installation, combined with unchanged components and paste, conclusively points to a reflow process issue, not a component quality issue.

The physics exclusion is based on the tombstoning mechanism and the temporal correlation. Tombstoning occurs when the surface tension forces of molten solder on the two pads are unbalanced: the pad that wets first pulls the component toward it before the second pad's solder melts. The torque is tau = F_wetting * L_component/2, where F_wetting is the surface tension force gamma * perimeter_contact. For the component metallization to cause tombstoning, one terminal would need significantly different wettability — but this would be a property of the component lot and would be present regardless of which oven is used.

The fact that the same components had < 0.5% tombstoning in the previous oven and 6% in the new oven proves the components are not the root cause. A metallization defect is a fixed physical property of the component — it does not change when the oven changes. The 12x increase in tombstoning rate, perfectly correlated with the oven change, points definitively to the reflow profile.

The actual mechanism is a thermal gradient across the board created by the new oven's heating zone configuration. Tombstoning is most sensitive to the temperature difference between the two pads of a small component at the moment solder transitions from solid to liquid (183C for Sn63/Pb37, 217-220C for SAC305). If one pad reaches liquidus 1-2 seconds before the other, the surface tension on the molten side creates an unbalanced torque that lifts the component. The new oven likely has a different zone length, blower configuration, or conveyor speed that creates a larger lateral thermal gradient across the board compared to the previous oven.

For 0201 components (0.6 mm x 0.3 mm), even a 1-2C/mm lateral gradient is sufficient to create the time offset for tombstoning, because the component is small enough that one pad can reach liquidus while the other is still below it.

To fix, profile the new oven with a thermocouple board that has sensors on both pads of critical 0201 locations. Compare the delta_T between pads to the old oven's profile. Adjust zone temperatures, conveyor speed, or blower settings to minimize the lateral gradient during the soak-to-reflow transition. Target delta_T < 3C between any two pads on the board at liquidus transition.

#### Sample 2 — in_circuit_test (319 words)

**Instruction:**

> Production test results for 200 consecutive boards show an abrupt transition: boards 1-147 all pass, then boards 148-200 all fail. The 53 failing boards span 4 different lot codes, 2 board revisions (Rev C and Rev D), and components from 3 different suppliers. The failure mode is consistent: all boards fail the 3.3V rail voltage measurement with readings 120-150 mV above the expected 3.300V. Is there a systematic component issue affecting the voltage regulator across multiple suppliers simultaneously?

**Response:**

This is not a hardware defect. The pattern conclusively rules out a component-level issue and points to a test system calibration failure.

The physics exclusion is based on the population statistics. A genuine hardware defect — whether a voltage regulator with shifted output, a feedback resistor with wrong value, or a reference voltage drift — would be correlated with a specific component lot, supplier, or date code. The fact that 53 consecutively-tested boards from 4 different lot codes, 2 board revisions, and 3 different suppliers all fail identically is statistically impossible for independent hardware defects. The probability of independent component failures aligning perfectly with a serial number boundary (not a lot boundary) is effectively zero.

Moreover, the failure signature is a consistent positive offset (120-150 mV above nominal) rather than a random spread. If multiple independent voltage regulators had drifted, each would drift by a different amount determined by its individual component tolerances, temperature coefficient, and aging. A tight cluster of offsets all in the same direction indicates a systematic measurement error, not a population of hardware defects.

The actual cause is test system calibration data corruption. The abrupt onset at board 148 — not at a lot boundary, shift change, or material change, but at a specific serial number — indicates an event in the test system, not in the product. Most likely, the test system experienced a crash, power interruption, or software error around the time board 147 was tested, and the calibration reference values for the 3.3V measurement channel were corrupted or reset to defaults. The 120-150 mV offset represents the uncalibrated measurement error of the DMM or ADC channel in the test fixture.

To confirm, measure one of the 'failing' boards with an independent calibrated multimeter. If the 3.3V rail reads 3.300V within spec, the test system is at fault. Recalibrate the measurement channel using the golden board reference standard and retest the 53 boards.
